Runtack

On long mountain runs I like carrying just plain water: beyond hydration you can soak a bandanna against the heat, or clean out a wound, and it's easier to refill at a stream than trying to mess around with powder mix. So I go to food bars for calories and electrolytes, but as the miles stack up commercial products would get expensive. I started making my own, and from time to time people ask me for the recipe, here it is:

  • Â½ c. quick oats
  • Â¼ t. salt
  • 1/16 t. "salt substitute" (KCl)
  • 1 T sugar
  • Â½ c. water
  1. Mix everything together and microwave on high one minute.
  2. Mix again to really get it gelatinous and uniform.
  3. Spread thinly on a baking sheet, bake at 400Â°F about 8 minutes.
  4. Take the baking sheet out of the oven, cut into quarters, flip, and return to oven.
  5. Continue baking maybe 6 minutes or so, then turn off the heat and prop the oven door to continue drying.

I usually let it sit out under a towel overnight to dry more before bringing it on a run the next morning. The goal is a texture that's rubbery on the inside and crispy on the outside, which I find holds up well in the pack but is easy to eat. Leftovers I freeze, then take out to thaw the night before. It'll get moldy if you just leave it out more than a couple days.

That recipe makes 4 bars, sized for 1 bar per hour of exercise. It can easily be scaled up for more but the measurements get futzy if you try scaling down. The oatmeal has pretty much exactly the right carb:protein ratio for long distances according to some numbers I got from The Lore of Running by Dr. Noakes. It's also pretty bland, which I find to be welcome as the hours stack up.

Unholy Matrimony

Brew a cup or pot of good quality tea, then reserve some in a sealed container and refrigerate. When it's chilled, mix your favorite good whisky and the tea in a 1:1 ratio.

The name comes because, to purists of either the tea or the whisky, mixing them like this is a sacrilege. But I find their flavors don't step on each other at all, rather they complement perfectly. My go-to mix is dragonwell (longjing) tea and Highland Park whisky. I can imagine (but haven't yet tried) that lapsang souchong and something like Laphroaig would be a fine mix too.
